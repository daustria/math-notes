Contour Integration¶

We will have a quick, simple discussion on contour integrals and how they throw light on the behaviour of holomorphic functions. Not intended to be a complete discussion, reader is expected to have acquaintance with contour integration already.

Note

A domain \(D\) will refer to an open connected subset of the complex plane. Functions will usually have the form \(f: D \to \mathbb{C}\) for some domain D.

Theorem

Let \(f\) be continuous in a domain D. The following are equivalent:

  1. \(f\) has an antiderivative \(F\) in D

  2. If \(\Gamma\) is a loop contour, \(\int_{\Gamma} f(z)\,dz=0\).

  3. Contour integrals of \(f\) are path independent.

We will prove (sketch) \(1 \implies 2 \implies 3 \implies 1\).

\(1 \implies 2\) is a version of the fundamental theorem of calculus. For the sake of presentation assume that \(\Gamma\) is a directed smooth curve (if not, run the proof on each of its components). Hence its parameterization \(z(t) : [a,b] \to \mathbb{C}\) is continuously differentiable. By the chain rule we have

\[\frac{d}{dt} F(z(t)) = f(z(t))z'(t)\]

and so

\[\int_{\Gamma}f(z)\,dz = \int_a^b f(z(t))z'(t)\,dt = F(b) - F(a).\]

where the last equation is just an invocation of a small extension to the fundamental theorem of calculus for real variables.

For \(2 \implies 3\) let \(z,w\) be two points in our domain. Let \(\gamma_1, \gamma_2\) be two distinct paths (contours) from \(z\) to \(w\), entirely contained in \(D\). Let \(\Gamma\) be the contour formed by appending \(-\gamma_2\) to \(\gamma_1\). Then by hypothesis the integral of \(f\) over \(\Gamma\) vanishes. This is exactly what we want since

\[0 = \int_{\Gamma} f = \int_{\gamma_1}f - \int_{\gamma_2}f\]

For \(3 \implies 1\) fix a point \(x\) in \(D\) and define \(F(z) = \int_x^z f(s)\,ds\) where the notation indicates integrating over a straight line from \(x\) to \(z\). We must show that \(F\) is differentiable and its derivative is \(f\), which can be done directly. Notice that \(x \to z+w \to z \to x\) is a loop integral. So by our hypothesis we have

\[F(z+w) - F(z) = \int_x^{z+w} f - \int_x^z f = \int_{z}^{z+w} f\]

So the proof is done if we can show

\[\frac{1}{w} \int_{z}^{z+w} f(s)\,ds \to f(z)\]

as \(w \to 0\). Parameterize the contour as \(z+wt\) for \(0 \leq t \leq 1\). Then the integral becomes

\[\frac{1}{w} \int_0^1 f(z+wt)w\,dt = \int_0^1 f(z+wt)\,dt.\]

Fianlly we have

\[\int_0^1 f(z+wt)\,dt - f(z) = \int_0^1 f(z+wt)-f(z)\,dt \to 0\]

as \(w \to 0\) \(\blacksquare\).
