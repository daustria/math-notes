Integration and Differentiability¶

Warning

still largely a work in progress

Holomorphic or complex-differentiable functions are expressible as their Taylor series.

Theorem

Let \(f\) be analytic in a disk \(|z-a|<R\) centred at \(a\). Then the Taylor series \(T_a(z)\) for \(f\) centred at \(a\) converges pointwise on this disk to \(f\). Namely, for each \(z\) in this disk we have

\[T_a(z) := \sum_{n \geq 0} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} (z-a)^n = f(z).\]

We will start from the Taylor series \(T_a(z)\) and show it is equal to \(f(z)\). We will integrate over a circle centred at \(a\), properly containing \(z\). By Cauchy’s integral formula,

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} T_a(z) &= \sum_{n \geq 0} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} (z-a)^n \\ &= \sum_{n \geq 0} \frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint \frac{f(s)}{(s-a)^{n+1}} (z-a)^n ds \\ &= \frac{1}{2 \pi i} \sum_{n \geq 0} \oint \frac{f(s)}{s-a} \bigg(\frac{z-a}{s-a}\bigg)^n ds. \end{align}\end{split}\]

Notice that the series obtained from interchanging summation and integration is a geometric series, which converges uniformly on the circle (\(|z-a|/|s-a| < 1\) for \(s\) in the circle). This justifies the interchange of operations. Continuing, we have

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint \sum_{n \geq 0} \frac{f(s)}{s-a} \bigg(\frac{z-a}{s-a}\bigg)^n ds &= \frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint \frac{f(s)}{s-a} \frac{1}{1 - \frac{z-a}{s-a}}ds \\ &= \frac{1}{2 \pi i} \oint \frac{f(s)}{s-z}\,dz \\ &= f(z) \end{align}\end{split}\]

as required \(\blacksquare\).
